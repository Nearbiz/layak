## 2. The Problem

Malaysia has a hidden economic class — workers who earn well, work hard, and produce real value, but live in a parallel financial reality. They are barbers, kuih sellers, freelance tutors, mobile mechanics, home-based F&B operators. They get paid through TNG eWallet QR and DuitNow QR. Their income is real, consistent, and verifiable. **But to a bank, they are invisible.**

We call them the **trapped middle of the QR economy** — ~500,000 to 1 million Malaysians, earning RM30,000–150,000/year through QR-based payments. Too established to be ignored. Too small to economically incorporate. Too undocumented to access fair credit.

## 3. The Deeper Insight — The Witness Gap

The **naive framing**: "They have no proof of income." A sharp judge will reject this.

The **sharp framing**: Trusted credit requires a **third-party witness**.

Salaried workers have two witnesses — their employer (salary attestation) + EPF (deduction attestation). Banks don't trust the worker's word — they trust the witnesses. That's why salaried workers get loans at 5–7% in 30 minutes.

QR-economy workers have no witness:
- Self-declared income → banks don't trust it
- Voluntary i-Saraan EPF → shows savings, not earnings
- LHDN Borang B → chronically under-reported
- Raw bank statements → mixed personal/business, expensive to parse

Every one of these is the worker **witnessing themselves**. No independent attestor. So banks charge 12–18% interest — double what a salaried worker pays.

**This is not a documentation gap. It is a witness gap.**

### Why not just incorporate Sdn Bhd?

| Cost | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Incorporation (one-time) | RM1,000–2,500 |
| Company secretary (annual) | RM800–1,500/yr |
| Annual audit | RM2,000–5,000/yr |
| Annual return + accounting | RM2,500–6,500/yr |
| **Total ongoing** | **RM5,000–15,000/yr** |

For Kumar at RM112k/year: 5–13% of revenue on compliance overhead before any benefit. For a kuih seller at RM40k/year: economically unviable. The Sdn Bhd path works for doctors at RM500k+. **Not for barbers and hawkers.**

## 4. The Product — Three Layers

### Layer 1 — Collective Intelligence
TNG sees the entire QR economy. Aggregate, anonymized data shows what real earnings look like by trade, by zone, by tenure.

Kumar sees: *"Barbers in Brickfields earn an average RM4,800/month. You earn RM4,200. Top quartile is RM6,100."*

This calibrates the Layak Score honestly (we know what "good" actually looks like).

### Layer 2 — Layak Score (the credentials layer)
A standardized, signed, machine-readable income credential issued by TNG. Like an EPF statement, but for QR-economy work.

Includes: verified earnings history, consistency metrics, customer diversity, a numerical Layak Score (300–850).

Workers control consent — they choose which lenders to share with. Lenders consume via API the same way they consume EPF data today.

### Layer 3 — Productive-Asset Credit
BNPL specifically for income-generating tools — not consumer junk:
- Clipper sets and second chairs for barbers
- Ovens and packaging equipment for kuih sellers
- Cameras and lighting for photographers
- Motorcycles for mobile service workers

Repayment terms underwritten on expected income lift from the asset itself.

### The Flywheel
More workers earn through TNG → richer collective data → more accurate Layak Scores → cheaper credit unlocked → workers buy productive tools → workers earn more through TNG → loop tightens.

## 5. The Moat — Structural Neutrality

**What the moat is NOT:** Data exclusivity. DuitNow QR is a national rail. Every bank sees fragments of QR earnings. Claiming "only TNG can see this data" is factually wrong — a sharp judge will dismantle it.

**What the moat IS:** Incentive neutrality.

Maybank could build this. CIMB could build this. None of them will build it well — because each has a vested interest in steering credentialed workers toward their own lending products. **TNG is the only major QR-economy player that isn't a lender.** TNG's commercial interest is transaction volume, ecosystem stickiness, and fee revenue — not originating loans.

### The Plaid analogy
Plaid never claimed exclusivity on US bank data. Plaid's moat was being **first to standardize the credential format** that lenders consumed. Once Lending Club, Robinhood, and Venmo built integrations against Plaid, lock-in was on the consumer side of the API.

Layak's moat works the same way: once Maybank, CIMB, AEON Credit, BSN, and 5 micro-financing partners are wired in, other eWallets face a chicken-and-egg problem.

> Data exclusivity is brittle (someone with money can replicate). Structural incentive alignment is durable (Maybank can't become neutral without giving up its lending business).

## 8. Q&A Prep (Haziq drills all of these)

**Q1: Can't self-employed workers just self-declare income?**
A: They can, but lenders don't trust self-declarations — they pay 2× the interest rate. We're not solving the documentation problem — we're solving the trust problem. Salaried workers have third-party witnesses. QR-economy workers don't — until Layak.

**Q2: Why don't they just incorporate Sdn Bhd?**
A: Sdn Bhd compliance runs RM5–15k/year. For a worker earning RM80k, that's 6–18% of revenue before any benefit. We serve the trapped middle: too established to ignore, too small to incorporate.

**Q3: Doesn't TNG already do this with GOpinjam?**
A: GOpinjam is captive — TNG to user, CIMB as lender. Great product, but a vertical channel with a ceiling. Layak is horizontal — TNG as neutral credentials issuer for every lender. GOpinjam becomes Layak's first integration, not its competitor.

**Q4: What stops Maybank/CIMB from building this?**
A: They can build a Maybank-branded credential, but lenders don't trust credentials issued by competing lenders. The credentials market needs a neutral issuer — that's TNG's structural advantage.

**Q5: DuitNow QR is shared — why is TNG the witness?**
A: TNG isn't the only witness — but TNG is the *right one to issue the standard*. Every bank sees only their own merchant slice. None has built credential infrastructure because their incentives don't align. TNG is incentive-aligned to be neutral. First to standardize wins.

**Q6: What about Bank Negara compliance?**
A: Layak is a credentialing layer, not a credit bureau. We issue verified income receipts with worker consent, that lenders use as one input to their existing scoring. Same regulatory posture as Plaid: data conduit, not regulated entity.

**Q7: Privacy — does the worker consent? Can they revoke?**
A: Workers see their own data. Lenders see only credentials a worker chooses to share, time-bound and revocable. TNG sees the market in aggregate — never individual non-customers.

**Q8: What if the worker uses multiple eWallets?**
A: v1 is TNG-native — that's where the data is richest. The single-wallet view is already a 10× improvement over no witness baseline. v2 we federate via DuitNow QR data sharing standards.

**Q9: CCRIS and CTOS already aggregate credit data — why do we need this?**
A: CCRIS/CTOS see existing credit performance. They don't see eWallet earnings. Layak is complementary: lenders use both. CCRIS for credit history, Layak for verified income.

**Q10: How big is the actual market?**
A: 500,000 QR-economy workers earning RM3,000+/month — conservative, defensible. That's a billion-ringgit lending TAM. Layer in BNPL margin and analytics licensing — 9-figure ARR by year five.
